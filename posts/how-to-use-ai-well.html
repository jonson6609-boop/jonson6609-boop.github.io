How to use AI tools well: 5 small habits that make a big difference

Published July 19, 2026 · 6 min read · By Maya Reyes

Most disappointing results from AI tools don't come from a bad model — they come from a vague ask. The good news is the fix is a handful of small habits, not a technical skill. Here are the five that make the biggest difference.

1. Say what "good" looks like, not just the topic

"Write about productivity" gives the tool almost nothing to aim at. "Write a 300-word list of three productivity tips for someone who works from home, in a casual tone" gives it a target. The more specific the request, the less guessing the tool has to do.

2. Give it the context a colleague would need

If you'd need to explain something to a new coworker before they could help you, the AI needs that too — your audience, your constraints, what you've already tried. Skipping context is the single biggest reason answers come back too generic to use.

3. Treat the first answer as a draft, not a final one

The real advantage of these tools is how cheap it is to ask again. If the first response is close but not quite right, say exactly what's off — "shorter," "more casual," "cut the third point" — instead of starting over. Refining is almost always faster than re-explaining from scratch.

4. Verify anything that matters before you rely on it

For facts, figures, quotes, or anything with real consequences — a number in a report, a claim in an email — double check it against a real source. AI tools can sound confident and still be wrong, so treat confident-sounding output as a draft to verify, not a citation to trust outright.

5. Keep sensitive information out of casual chats

Don't paste passwords, financial account numbers, or other sensitive personal data into a general-purpose chat tool unless you understand exactly how that tool handles your data. When in doubt, describe the situation without the sensitive specifics.

None of this is about finding a clever trick. It's closer to how you'd brief a new assistant: be specific, give context, and check the important stuff before you use it.

Start with just one

If you only adopt one habit from this list, make it the first one — being specific about what "good" looks like. It's the smallest change here, and it's usually the one that fixes the most disappointing results.
