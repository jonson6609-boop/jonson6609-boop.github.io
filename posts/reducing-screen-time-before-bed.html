Reducing screen time before bed, without willpower

Published July 18, 2026 · 5 min read · By Jonah Pierce

Nobody fails at "no phones before bed" because they lack self-control. They fail because the plan relies on willpower at exactly the moment willpower is lowest — the end of a long day. The fix isn't more discipline. It's making the phone slightly harder to reach than the alternative.

1. Charge your phone outside the bedroom

This one change does more work than any app or setting. If your phone isn't within arm's reach, "just checking one thing" stops being a reflex and becomes a decision — and most people, once they'd have to get up for it, decide it can wait until morning.

2. Put a real book where your phone used to sit

Willpower struggles to fight "nothing," but it barely has to work against "something already there." A book, even one you don't finish, gives your hands something to reach for instead — so you're not choosing between phone and boredom, just between two easy options.

3. Set one alarm to end the evening, not to start the morning

Most people set a wake-up alarm and nothing else, so nights have no natural endpoint. A second alarm — say, 45 minutes before you want to be asleep — acts as a nudge that the day is wrapping up, before you're already deep in a scroll.

The goal isn't zero screen time. It's making the off-ramp slightly easier to take than the on-ramp, right at the point in the day when you have the least energy to fight it.

Why removing friction beats adding rules

Screen-time rules that rely on remembering to stop tend to quietly disappear within a week, because they ask the most of you exactly when you have the least to give. Moving the phone, swapping in a book, and setting one honest alarm all work before the decision point even arrives — so there's nothing left to resist in the moment.

Start with just the charging spot. It's the smallest change here, and usually the one that matters most.
