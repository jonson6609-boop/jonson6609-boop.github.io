The ten-minute subscription audit that pays for itself

Published July 22, 2026 · 5 min read · By Maya Reyes

Subscriptions are designed to be forgotten. Small monthly charges, spread across a dozen different apps and services, rarely show up as one obvious problem — they just quietly add up in the background. This ten-minute audit finds them without needing a spreadsheet or a budgeting app.

Step 1: Open your bank statement, not your memory

Don't try to list your subscriptions from memory — most people can name three or four and forget the rest. Instead, open your last two months of bank or card transactions on your phone. That's the full, honest list.

Step 2: Scroll and circle anything recurring

Look for the same amount, or the same merchant name, appearing more than once. Streaming services, apps, storage plans, memberships — anything charging you on autopilot. You're not judging them yet, just spotting them.

Step 3: Ask one honest question per item

For each one: "Did I use this in the last month?" Not "might I use it someday" — just the last month, honestly. If the answer is no, it's a candidate to cancel. If you're not sure what a charge even is, that's an even stronger signal.

Step 4: Cancel the clear no's today, not "this weekend"

Subscriptions you plan to cancel later tend to survive another billing cycle, then another. Cancel the obvious ones the moment you spot them — most services let you do it in under a minute from your phone.

You're not trying to cut everything. You're trying to stop paying for things you've already stopped using — those two are easy to confuse when the charge is small.

Why small subscriptions add up more than they feel like they should

A single $8 or $12 monthly charge feels trivial in isolation, which is exactly why it survives. It's rarely one subscription that's the problem — it's four or five small ones running quietly at once. Finding them all in one sitting, rather than one at a time, is what makes this worth doing.

Set a reminder to repeat this audit every three months. Subscriptions have a way of creeping back in.
