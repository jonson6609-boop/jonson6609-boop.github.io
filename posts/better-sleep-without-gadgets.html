Better sleep, without buying a single gadget

Published July 14, 2026 · 7 min read · By Jonah Pierce

The sleep-tech industry would like you to believe good sleep requires a $300 ring or a mattress with an app. Most of the highest-impact changes cost nothing and take a week to notice.

1. Fix your wake time, not your bedtime

Chasing an earlier bedtime rarely works, because you can't force sleep on demand. A consistent wake time — same time every day, weekends included — is what actually stabilizes your body clock. Bedtime tends to fall into place on its own within a week or two.

2. Get outside light in the first hour

Ten minutes outside, or even by a bright window, shortly after waking helps anchor your circadian rhythm and makes it easier to feel tired at the right time that night. No blue-light glasses or lamps required — ordinary daylight does this better than any device.

3. Set a "screens down" time, not a bedtime

Rather than fighting to fall asleep earlier, pick a time to stop looking at screens — 45 minutes before you actually plan to sleep. It's a smaller ask than an entire digital detox, and it still cuts the alertness that scrolling tends to produce right before bed.

4. Keep the bedroom slightly cooler than feels comfortable

Body temperature needs to drop for sleep to kick in, and a room that's a touch cool helps that happen faster. Most people find somewhere in the mid-60s Fahrenheit (about 18–19°C) works well, though it's worth adjusting to what actually helps you fall asleep faster.

None of these require tracking anything. That's deliberate — the moment sleep becomes a project with data to check, it tends to become a source of stress rather than rest.

Give it a week before judging it

Circadian adjustments aren't instant. A consistent wake time, in particular, can take several days to show its effect. Try all four for one week straight before deciding whether they're working — most people notice a difference by day five or six.
