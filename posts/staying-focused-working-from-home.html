Staying focused while working from home

Published July 17, 2026 · 5 min read · By Maya Reyes

The hardest part of working from home isn't motivation — it's that your brain never gets a clear signal for "now I'm working" versus "now I'm not." The same couch, the same kitchen, the same phone, all day. These four changes fix that signal problem without requiring a home office makeover.

1. Give yourself a fake commute

A short walk before you sit down to work — even five minutes around the block — gives your brain a clear boundary between "home mode" and "work mode." The same walk in reverse at the end of the day helps you actually switch off, instead of drifting from your laptop straight into dinner.

2. Work from one seat, not wherever's comfortable

Rotating between the couch, the bed, and the kitchen table feels flexible, but it quietly erases the boundary between rest and work. Pick one spot as your "work seat," even if it's just one side of the kitchen table, and try to keep it just for work.

3. Turn off notifications in blocks, not all day

Trying to silence your phone completely usually fails within a day. Instead, pick two or three specific blocks — say, 9–11am and 2–4pm — where notifications go off. Outside those blocks, check freely. It's a smaller ask, so it actually sticks.

4. End the day with a two-minute shutdown

Before closing your laptop, write one line: what you finished, and the one thing to start with tomorrow. It takes two minutes, but it stops work from quietly following you into the evening, since your brain isn't left holding an open loop.

None of these require an app, a planner, or a redesigned office. They work by giving your brain a clear line between "on" and "off" — which is the thing a shared space usually blurs.

Start with just one

Trying all four at once usually backfires. Pick whichever feels most doable this week — for most people, the fake commute or the shutdown routine is the easiest to start with — and add the others once it feels automatic.
